01 / Market Context

The Opportunity

Most college quiz competitions are ad-hoc—organized by enthusiasts, run once annually, disappear when organizers graduate. Few become institutionalized or attract college funding. Even fewer sustain across multiple years or spawn derivative events.

Bodh emerged from recognizing a pattern: students enjoy competitive intellectual platforms, but infrastructure (registration, scoring, engagement) is usually amateur. The opportunity: build quiz show as a systems-driven platform, not one-off event. If execution worked once, could it scale? Could it attract institutional support?

This required two things: (1) Prove the concept (execution excellence first time), (2) Build IT infrastructure that made repetition easy. Most quiz organizers skip #2 and burn out. Bodh focused on infrastructure from the start.

02 / The Journey

From Contributor to Founder

Sep–Dec 2021: Started as backend volunteer on Q'triosity, the college's small annual quiz show. Got exposure to event operations, registration systems, scoring. Saw opportunities for improvement.

Key decision: Rather than just run events, build systems that made execution repeatable. IT infrastructure became the differentiator—registration automation, scoring frameworks, analytics, social integration.

04 / The Systems

Infrastructure-First Platform Design

System 1: Registration & Participant Management
Automated registration system instead of manual signups. Participants could self-register, receive confirmations, access event information. Enabled scaling from 50 to 500+ participants per event without proportional admin burden. Captured participant data for follow-up, community building, and future event insights.
System 2: Scoring & Leaderboard
Standardized scoring framework and real-time leaderboard. Made event execution faster (judges no longer manually tracked scores) and more transparent (participants could see live rankings). Reduced scoring disputes and improved participant engagement.
System 3: Social Media & Analytics
Integrated social media promotion with event infrastructure. Tracked engagement metrics, post reach, and community growth across events. Used analytics to refine future event positioning and promotion strategy. Made Bodh visible beyond just participants—built institutional brand.
System 4: Operational Documentation
Documented all IT processes: setup checklists, troubleshooting guides, standard operating procedures. Enabled continuity—future organizers could run events without starting from scratch. Reduced dependency on any single person's knowledge.

06 / Key Challenges & Decisions

Building a Sustainable Platform

The Challenge

  • Started as small backend volunteer role (Q'triosity)
  • Opportunity to expand, but no existing infrastructure
  • Risk: become just another one-off event that disappears
  • Need to convince college leadership to invest in recurring platform
  • Scaling meant dealing with complexity (more events, more participants, more moving parts)

The Approach

  • Built IT infrastructure first, before scaling events
  • Made founding proposal to college: "Here's why this works, here's how we scale"
  • Focused on systems that reduced human burden (automation, documentation)
  • Measured success through metrics (attendance, engagement, brand reach) not just "did event happen"
  • Documented everything to ensure continuity beyond founder departure

08 / Learnings

What Founding Bodh Taught Me

Infrastructure Enables Scale. One-off events are volunteer-intensive. Repeatable events require systems. The difference between "we ran this once" and "we run this yearly" is infrastructure. Building for repetition from the start costs more upfront but enables sustainability.

Side Projects Can Become Institutions. Most side projects stay side projects because organizers don't think systemically. If you design for sustainability (documentation, processes, team distribution), side projects can attract institutional support and become recurring.

Proposing > Waiting for Permission. Didn't ask college "can we expand Q'triosity?" Proposed "here's what Bodh could be, here's why it works, here's how we execute." Came with solution, not problem. That's how you get resources.

Systems Don't Replace People, They Amplify Them. Automating registration doesn't remove the need for organizers. It removes the busywork, freeing organizers to focus on participant experience, event creativity, and strategic growth.

Measure What Matters. Success isn't "event happened." Success is: attendance growth, participant satisfaction, community engagement, brand reach, sustainability. Measure strategically to guide iteration.

Founder Dependency is a Bug, Not a Feature. Deliberately built systems and documented processes so platform could function without me. That's how institutions work—people come and go, but systems persist.
