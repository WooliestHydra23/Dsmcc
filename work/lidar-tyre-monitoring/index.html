LiDAR Tyre Monitoring

Product Innovation & Market Validation

A comprehensive exploration of IoT-enabled vehicle safety: researching LiDAR technology feasibility, defining the market opportunity, and building a go-to-market strategy for an emerging product category.

My Role

Market Research, Product Strategy, Technical Feasibility Analysis, Customer Research

Focus Areas

Technology validation, market sizing, customer personas, competitive analysis, GTM strategy

Industry

Fleet Management, Vehicle Safety, IoT, Automotive, Logistics

Product Category

IoT Sensor System, Real-time Monitoring, Predictive Maintenance

01 / Why This Mattered

The Stakes

Tyre failure is a leading cause of vehicle accidents, fleet downtime, and maintenance costs. Commercial fleets—long-haul trucking, logistics, public transit—lose millions annually to unplanned tyre failures and reactive replacements.

Emerging technologies like LiDAR offer potential for real-time condition monitoring: detecting wear patterns, pressure anomalies, structural damage—before failure occurs. But potential is not market. The question was: could this become a viable, validated product category?

This project aimed to de-risk that uncertainty through research, validation, and strategic analysis.

02 / The Challenge

What Needed Answering

The core question: Is this a real business opportunity? But that breaks down into harder questions:

  • Technology: Is LiDAR actually viable for tyre monitoring? What are the technical constraints, sensor costs, accuracy benchmarks, and real-world limitations?
  • Market: Who are the actual buyers? Fleet operators? Tyre manufacturers? Insurance companies? OEMs? Different buyers = different markets.
  • Competition: Why haven't existing solutions solved this? What's the whitespace where this product could differentiate?
  • Business Model: Hardware sale? Subscription? Integrated service? Partnership? The model directly impacts viability.
03 / My Research System

How I De-Risked the Uncertainty

01 / Technology Feasibility
Researched LiDAR sensor specifications, cost trajectories, accuracy benchmarks, and real-world constraints. Identified what works and what doesn't. Found the gap between theoretical capability and field reality.
02 / Market Sizing & Definition
Estimated addressable market by segment: commercial fleet size, average tyre costs, downtime losses, willingness-to-pay for prevention. Built bottom-up market sizing, not top-down guesses.
03 / Customer Understanding
Developed detailed personas for different buyer types (Fleet Operations Manager, Safety Director, Procurement Lead, Insurance Partner). Mapped decision criteria, objections, and pain points for each.
04 / Competitive Landscape
Analyzed existing tyre monitoring solutions (pressure-based, temperature sensors, visual inspection). Identified their limitations and the gap where LiDAR could differentiate.
05 / Product & GTM Strategy
Synthesized research into product concept, feature roadmap, value proposition by persona, and go-to-market strategy. Built a credible narrative that connects technology → market → business model.

05 / Before & After

What Changed Through This Research

Before

  • Hypothesis: LiDAR could monitor tyres. But was it viable? Nobody knew for certain.
  • Market assumption: "Large fleets would want this." But who? Trucking? Transit? Taxis? Different customer = different value prop.
  • Competition: Assumed existing solutions were weak. But in what specific ways? Where could differentiation actually exist?
  • Business case: Scattered ideas about hardware, subscriptions, partnerships. No clear model.

After

  • Technology validated: LiDAR is viable. Identified the specific sensors, cost points, integration pathways, and constraints that matter.
  • Market clarified: Different fleet segments have different needs. Long-haul trucking has different pain than public transit. Identified early adopter segment.
  • Competition understood: Existing solutions focus on pressure or temperature. The gap: real-time structural damage detection. That's where differentiation lives.
  • Business model defined: Subscription + hardware makes sense. Early adopter partnerships as entry strategy. Pricing benchmarked.
06 / Impact

What This Enabled

De-Risked the Opportunity. Eliminated hypothesis through research. Provided confidence for go/no-go decision on product investment.

Identified the Real Constraints. Technology risk, market risk, and competitive risk all became specific and addressable, not abstract.

Clarified the Entry Point. Instead of "sell to all fleets," identified a specific early adopter segment with validated pain and willingness to pay.

Aligned Leadership. Product, business, and leadership teams moved from "interesting idea" to "validated opportunity with specific go-to-market approach."

Created a Roadmap. Not just for product development, but for market validation, customer discovery, partnership, and launch timing.

My role was not to engineer the LiDAR sensor or build the product. My role was to research the opportunity, validate the market, and build the strategic case for moving forward. That clarity is what made the research valuable.

08 / Reflection

What I Learned

Technology ≠ Opportunity. A brilliant technology solution means nothing if nobody will pay for it. Market validation comes before engineering validation.

The Entry Point Matters Most. A $10B addressable market is irrelevant if you can't reach it. The real question: where is your first customer? What's the smallest viable market entry point?

Willingness-to-Pay Beats Problem Acknowledgment. Customers might agree a problem exists. But will they actually buy a solution? Will they change vendors for it? That distinction is critical.

Research Reduces Risk Asymmetrically. Spending time upfront identifying technical, market, and competitive risks saves massive cost during execution. The earlier you find the constraints, the better.
