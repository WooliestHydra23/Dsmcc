LiDAR Tyre Monitoring

Product Innovation • Market Research • Go-to-Market Strategy

01 / Context

The Problem Space

Tyre failure is a leading cause of vehicle accidents, fleet downtime, and maintenance costs. Commercial fleets—long-haul trucking, logistics, public transit—lose millions annually to unplanned tyre failures and expensive reactive replacements.

Emerging LiDAR technology (used in autonomous vehicles and robotics) offers potential for real-time tyre condition monitoring: detecting wear patterns, pressure anomalies, structural damage—before failure occurs. The question: could this become a viable product and market?

02 / Problem

What Needed Solving

Technology Validation: Is LiDAR actually viable for tyre monitoring? What are the technical constraints, sensor costs, and real-world accuracy rates?

Market Definition: Who are the actual buyers? Tyre manufacturers? Fleet operators? Insurance companies? Vehicle OEMs? Different buyers = different value propositions.

Business Model Uncertainty: Hardware sale? Subscription service? Integrated into existing telematics? Insurance partnership? The model shapes viability.

Competitive Landscape: What existing solutions address this? Why haven't they solved it? What's the whitespace?

Go-to-Market Path: Even if viable, how do you reach the market? What's the entry point? Early adopter segment?

03 / My Role

What I Researched & Delivered

Technology Feasibility Research: Investigated LiDAR sensor specifications, cost trajectories, accuracy benchmarks. Identified technical constraints (weather, speed ranges, installation complexity) and opportunities (integration with existing vehicle systems).

Market Sizing: Estimated addressable market by segment: commercial fleet size, average tyre replacement costs, downtime losses, willingness to pay for prevention. Conducted secondary research on fleet management pain points.

Customer Persona Development: Identified primary buyers (Fleet Operations Manager, Safety Director, Procurement Lead) and secondary influencers (Maintenance Teams, Insurance Partners). Mapped decision criteria and objections for each.

Competitive Landscape Analysis: Researched existing tyre monitoring solutions (pressure-based, temperature-based, visual inspection). Identified gaps (real-time damage detection, integration friction, cost barriers) where LiDAR could differentiate.

Product Concept Development: Designed product concept, feature roadmap, and value proposition for primary customer segment. Created pitch presentation and use case scenarios.

Go-to-Market Strategy: Recommended entry point (early adopter fleets with high downtime costs), partnership opportunities (tyre manufacturers, fleet telematics providers), and pricing model (subscription + hardware).

04 / Process

How I Approached It

Research Design: Multi-modal research: technical (LiDAR sensor specs, benchmarks), market (fleet size data, cost analysis), competitive (existing solutions), and customer (interviews, pain point analysis).

Technology Validation: Reviewed academic papers on LiDAR for material inspection. Consulted with hardware engineers on feasibility and cost. Identified sensor options and integration pathways.

Market Analysis: Pulled industry data on fleet sizes, tyre replacement rates, downtime costs. Estimated willingness-to-pay by analyzing insurance savings from accident prevention.

Customer Research: Interviewed fleet operators, maintenance directors, and insurance companies to validate pain points and decision criteria. Mapped customer journey from problem awareness to purchase.

Concept Development: Synthesized research into product concept (hardware device + cloud dashboard), feature roadmap (MVP → full capability), and pitch narrative for investors/stakeholders.

05 / Output

What Was Delivered

Feasibility Report: 20+ page document on technology viability, sensor options, cost analysis, integration pathways, and technical roadmap.

Market Sizing Analysis: Addressable market estimates by geography and segment. Competitive positioning map. Pricing benchmarks from adjacent markets.

Customer Personas & Insights: Detailed personas (Fleet Operations Manager, Safety Director, Procurement Lead) with decision criteria, objections, and messaging recommendations.

Product Concept Document: Problem statement, solution overview, feature roadmap (MVP → Phase 2 → Phase 3), value proposition by persona, competitive differentiation.

Go-to-Market Strategy: Target customer segment (high-downtime fleets), entry strategy (partnership with leading fleet operators), pricing model, and 18-month launch roadmap.

Pitch Presentation: Executive summary, market opportunity, product concept, team capabilities, financial projections, funding ask.

06 / Impact

What This Enabled

Strategic Clarity: Eliminated hypothesis—this is a real problem in a real market with proven customer willingness to pay. Provides confidence for go/no-go decision.

De-risked Investment: Identified key technical and market risks upfront. Provides roadmap for risk mitigation (pilot with early adopter fleet, partnership pathway, etc).

Accelerated Development: Product team has clear feature priorities based on customer value, not internal assumptions. Roadmap is customer-validated.

Sales Readiness: Go-to-market team has customer personas, messaging, and value proposition ready for day one. Knows who to target and how to position.

Investor Conversation: Market size, customer validation, competitive differentiation, and go-to-market clarity provide confidence for fundraising or board approval.

08 / Reflection

What I Learned

Technology ≠ Market: LiDAR is impressive, but the product only matters if it solves a real customer problem that customers will pay for. Tech-first thinking leads to expensive failures. Customer-first thinking leads to viable products.

Market Entry Beats Market Size: A $10 billion market is irrelevant if your entry point doesn't exist. Finding the early adopter segment, understanding their specific pain, and designing for them beats trying to address a massive market from day one.

Willingness-to-Pay is Everything: Customers might acknowledge a problem, but will they actually buy a solution? Understanding the buying decision (who decides, what's the budget, when does money move) matters more than problem acknowledgment.

Risk Mitigation Starts in Research: Spending time upfront identifying technical risks (sensor cost, accuracy, integration), market risks (buyer fragmentation, adoption resistance), and competitive risks (existing solutions improving) saves massive cost during execution.
